Restart · a case for learned grid restoration

Ukraine restarts its grid wrong every week. There is a way to do it right.

An AI advisor that learns the safest sequence to re-energize feeders after a blackout — trained on a digital twin of Ukraine's actual network, and presented to operators with a one-line explanation for every recommendation.

After every blackout, an operator must re-energize a feeder. With every thermostat, fridge, and heater off, demand snaps back all at once and trips the upstream breaker — blackout again. The current playbook is a paper procedure written for a grid that no longer exists. Operators perform it under air-raid sirens, with degraded telemetry, sometimes from candlelight.

The idea

A reinforcement-learning policy that recommends which segment to energize next, at what voltage ramp, after what dwell time. The policy is trained against millions of synthetic blackout scenarios on a calibrated digital twin of Ukrainian topology. Every recommendation is shown to a human operator with an explanation. Never autonomous.

Restoration is the one grid problem where seconds of delay cost megawatt-hours, and where the right action depends on weather, time, and damage. That is exactly the regime where RL beats both human procedures and classical optimization.

How it works

  1. Build a twin of a real Ukrainian sub-region. One oblast's 35 / 10 kV network modeled in OpenDSS. Calibrated against ≥18 months of historical SCADA traces. Loads modeled as a population of equivalent-thermal-parameter houses, so cold-load behavior emerges rather than being hard-coded.
  2. Generate scenarios. Synthesize millions of blackout starting states across season, ambient temperature, outage duration, and damage pattern. This dataset — restoration under realistic adversarial damage — exists nowhere else.
  3. Train a policy. PPO with action masking in Stable-Baselines3. Curriculum from single feeder to full regional topology. Reward = load restored, penalized for trips, voltage violations, and time. An explainer is attached at training time, not bolted on after.
  4. Validate in hardware. Replay the policy on Typhoon HIL in real time. This is the sim-to-real check: does it still beat the baseline when load parameters drift and measurement noise is added?
  5. Shadow-mode beside the operator. The console shows the agent's recommendation next to the operator's intended action. The operator decides. Every disagreement becomes a training example.
  6. Promote to advisory. After months of high agreement, the operator can accept a recommendation with one click. Two-person confirmation above a learned risk threshold. The system never becomes fully closed-loop.

Why this works where the alternatives don't

Paper procedures

Assume seasonal averages and stable topology. Ukraine in 2026 has neither.

Classical optimization

Requires re-formulating every time the grid changes shape. The grid changes shape every week.

Learned policy

Maps state → next action. Topology and damage are inputs, not assumptions. Retrains on its own disagreements.

Stages

stage one

Single-feeder proof

One 10 kV feeder in OpenDSS. The trained policy beats both the greedy baseline and the paper procedure on time-to-99% restoration and on cascading trips.

stage two

Regional twin

An entire oblast modeled and calibrated to historical SCADA. Policy curriculum-trained across seasons and damage patterns. Operator-facing explainer working.

stage three

Hardware-in-the-loop and shadow

Real-time validation on Typhoon HIL. The console runs live in a control room. Operators see recommendations, decide, and the disagreement log feeds the next training run.

stage four

Advisory with approval

One-click accept becomes available where the agent's risk score is low and the operator agrees. Two-person confirmation above the threshold. Always a human in the loop.

What this says

The worst column is +1 to +3 °C — counter-intuitively, the policy handles deep cold better than mild ambient, because deep cold produces more uniformly heavy CLPU it has already seen, while mild ambient gives variable inrush the policy guesses wrong on.

The path to 0% trip isn't "train more on the worst column." It's a safety shield: a forecasting head that vetoes any closure expected to cross 240 A. PPO chooses, the shield checks.

What this prevents

Every cascading trip during restoration is another hour without power. In a Ukrainian winter that is heating off for hospitals, water pumps stopped, dialysis interrupted, apartment-block boilers refusing to restart, and street-corner generators bleeding diesel reserves the city cannot replace. A measurable cut in cascading trips during restoration is not a metric — it is hours of heat returned to people in February.

Risks owned up to

Sim-to-real gap

Field load won't perfectly match the population we sampled. Handled with domain randomization during training and online fine-tuning from real disagreements.

Operator trust

One visibly wrong recommendation costs a year of credibility. Handled by a long shadow period, an explanation attached to every recommendation, and no autonomous mode.

Data access

SCADA history is sensitive. Handled by training on-prem inside the utility perimeter; no data leaves the building.

Topology drift

A re-routed substation is a new problem. Handled by conditioning the policy on a topology hash and meta-training across a distribution of topologies, so adaptation is fast.

The case in one line

Ukraine has the world's most demanding grid-restoration problem, the world's richest dataset for solving it, and tooling that didn't exist five years ago. The question is no longer whether this works — only whether someone builds it before the next winter.
