The 8 segments are no longer interchangeable residential blocks. Each carries a different load, a different CLPU shape, and a different criticality weight that the policy reads from its observation.

Same blackout, four orderings. Pick the weather.

Four scenarios; each replayed by greedy, sequential-d8, the priority PPO, and a panic-close simulation that energizes every breaker in a single step (worst-case operator transient). On this archetype mix the panic-close doesn't trip the upstream breaker — but it does eat ~85% of trip capacity in 30 seconds, vs the priority PPO's ~62%. The story isn't "shield prevents trips here"; it's headroom. The priority PPO restores everything with two and a half times the safety margin of a panic close, and gets the hospital online first.

What this changes

The original Phase-0 result locked a technical claim: 0% trips, 11.7 steps to 99% restored. The priority result locks a human claim: hospital online in 1.5 minutes; every critical segment energized within 4 minutes of the operator pressing start.

Both are true on the same physics, with the same 8-segment feeder, the same safety shield. What changed is the reward function and what the agent learned to optimize. The policy is unchanged in structure; only the values it now seeks have changed.

Three implications:

  • For an operator. The recommendation reads like a clinical decision — "hospital first because backup generator is at 30%." That's the framing they're accountable for.
  • For a regulator. The policy is auditable: every recommendation carries the criticality weight that drove it. There's no hidden value judgment baked into a paper procedure.
  • For a partner conversation. The pitch is now "we restore hospitals first, in 1.5 minutes," not "we restore feeders fast." That's the conversation a Ukrenergo R&D person actually has.
